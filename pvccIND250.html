In our first class, we learned how to adjust our orientation, create new sketches, and set our default unit of measurement in Fusion 360. I found it extremely helpful to go over the basics considering we will be using this software for another class.

28 Jan 2019

We used a sketch of a caliper to learn how to create tool paths. It felt like a ton of steps, but I felt fairly good about the process, as I was able to reference previous experience with the Carbide Create and VCarve Pro.

4 Feb 2019

Review of creating tool paths in Fusion 360 with a Universal Laser Systems post processor.

11 Feb 2019

The question that was prompted to us today was, "What is the difference between work coordinate system and the machine coordinate offset?" My initial thought is that the work coordinate system is related to the CAD/CAM process, whereas the machine coordinate offset is related to the physical machine's position related to the stock material. We experimented with depth per pass, spindle speed, feedrate, and plunge rate. During our experiment, I documented on the white board above our Nomad 833 Pro.

18 Feb 2019

Today, we looked at the g-code that we generated from Fusion 360 for our Nomad 883 Pro. Some codes were obvious such as S6000, being the spindle speed. Some were less obvious such as, G90.1 and G91.1, which relates to arc distance mode. We used the Tormach Machine References.

25 Feb 2019

Part of today was generating CAM for a 2D piece. The piece given to use was a wrench, but we were allowed to choose anything after going through the process.

At this point, I had not cut my wheel piece for the Nomad 883. Dustin and I spent some time outside of class reviewing our CAM setup. Both of us had hesitation in regards to crashing the machine or breaking tooling. Going through the setup and operations with each other, allowed us to figure out what certain settings meant. One conformation that took place for me was stock orientation. When explaining the process, it clicked for me. In addition, I discovered/learned that the way Fusion understands the final depth of cut is related to the selection in the geometry tab. Once a geometry is selected, you can then make adjustments to the offsets in the heights tab. We started cutting our bolt wheel the same day, which included a facing operation for the bottom of the stock. We ended up running the facing operation twice, because we still had an uneven surface, and battled to keep our stock clamped into place. After eventually facing both sides, we decided to call it a day.

25 March 2019

This week was more about work flow, and learning proper tool usage. One of the items we spoke about were using HSS drill bits to at minimum predrill holes in order to save our end mills. In addition to CAM, making an item in CAD and going through work flow was just as meaningful to me.

1 April 2019

Today was an introduction to soft jaws, which I would define as dispensable aluminum clamps. The soft jaws would allow us to hold our stock material in the vice and give ourselves room to cut without ramming our tool into the vice. Ultimately, creating the soft jaws became a practical assessment of the procedures to create multiple setups and multiple cut operations. One of the benefits of the soft jaw project was discussing the work flow of cutting a part. Specifically, making a level reference point by using our facing opertion, and which side(s) should be faced earlier.

Our final project guideline was also introduced. At the time, I decided to design and cut a flip jig and threaded table for the Nomad 883 Pro desktop CNC machine. I wanted to do this project out of need for my lab at Community Middle, and for the PVCC lab.

8 April 2019

During this point in class, I was doing a combination of helping fellow classmates with creating CAM, and making alterations to my Nomad 883 Pro parts. I was still pretty hesitant to machine aluminum, I guess a fear of breaking a tool.

15 April 2019

Around this point in class, I changed my final project to picture frames, as I and another teacher were collaborating on a photography project. This was a good test for me to ensure I had a parametric design, and to design based on customer need. After designing four frames and cutting a prototype on the ShopBot Buddy out of 3/8 inch plywood, the teacher and I made some small but significant changes to the frame dimensions in order for the matboard to properly fit into the frame. I spent my class time assisting other students, and discussing project ideas.

23 April 2019

In my next steps of cutting picture frame, I walked a class of students through the process of securing stock onto ShopBot Buddy, homing the machine, loading .spb file, and cutting. The first job was only one frame. During the rest of the week, I put three frames in the same job. One thing I noticed while adding multiple frames to the same job, if setup is post processed and other projects are added later, the offsets are no longer accurate. Instead of being able to use the same stock and setup for my frames and exchange the frames for a new batch, I had to start a new file, load in my designs, then start my setup and opertations from scratch. A few times, I lost my coordinate position, this would happen if I paused the machine a few times while attempting to make a correction. In order to refind my position, I would pick a portion of the job that was already cut, and try to re-home the machine so that my pass above would align properly to the previous cut.

I also had multiple opportunities to help other students cut their projects on the Tormach PCNC 1100 and Tormach PCNC 700. After gaining confidence in measuring the tooling, and gaining confidence of seeing the machine exacute the gcode, I felt a lot better about cutting aluminum. A couple of times, a tool was damage, but normally it was mistake the student and I could figure out quickly. By the end of the class, I felt really comfortable setting up and cutting metal.
